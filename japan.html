Top Sights

Arashiyama

TArashiyama is the second-most important sightseeing district in Kyoto. It’s filled with temples and shrines, but the star attraction is the famed Arashiyama Bamboo Grove.

Dotonbori

The lively entertainment area of Dotonbori is Osaka’s most famous tourist destination and renowned for its gaudy neon lights, extravagant signage, and the enormous variety of restaurants and bars.

Inari Gates

It is famous for its thousands of vermilion torii gates, which straddle a network of trails behind its main buildings. The trails lead into the wooded forest of the sacred Mount Inari, which stands at 233 meters and belongs to the shrine grounds.

Kiyomizu dera

This most-visited temple in Kyoto was built to honor Kannon, the goddess of mercy. It's beautiful hillside location offers spectacular views of the city and valley below.

Nara

Nara is a gorgeous town that houses many temples through leafy woodland and is home to hundreds of very nosey and inquisitive deer- make sure you don't have any food in your pockets!

Osaka

Osaka (大阪, Ōsaka) is Japan's second largest metropolitan area after Tokyo. It has been the economic powerhouse of the Kansai Region for many centuries. The best thing to do here is to visit the castle.

Tea Ceremony

The Japanese tea ceremony, also called the Way of Tea, is a Japanese cultural activity involving the ceremonial preparation and presentation of matcha (抹茶), powdered green tea.

Tenryu-Ji Temple

Tenryuji (天龍寺, Tenryūji) is the most important temple in Kyoto's Arashiyama district. It was ranked first among the city's five great Zen temples, and is now registered as a world heritage site.
Transport

Japan has an efficient public transportation network, especially within metropolitan areas and between the large cities. Japanese public transportation is characterized by its punctuality, its superb service, and the large crowds of people using it. Although the trains are super fast, they can also be incredibly expensive, so consider getting a week/month tourist train pass

Food

Ramen

Ramen (egg noodles in a salty broth) is Japan’s favourite late-night meal. It’s also the perfect example of an imported dish that the Japanese have made completely and deliciously their own.

Okonomiyaki

It’s a savoury pancake filled with any number of things (but usually cabbage and pork) and topped with fish flakes, dried seaweed, mayonnaise and a Worcester-style sauce.

Sushi

Put simply, sushi is raw fish served on rice seasoned lightly with vinegar. It’s in the variety of flavours and textures that things get interesting.

Blog

What to do in a day

In the morning, head to the Arashiyama district of Kyoto to see the beautiful bamboo groves and the temple of your way. Then take the bus and head back to the city center and stop via the golden temple. Then, for lunch, make your way up to the top of the hill to the most famous temple in Kyoto for gorgeous views and even better streetfood. Then loop down into the Gion district where you can hope to see some geisha girls on their way to some appointments.

What to do in 48h

After coffee (and perhaps some toast and jam?), walk over to the Chion-in and Shore-in temples. You can spend hours staring up at these stunning works of architecture. Try not to get too caught up; there is a lot to see and plenty of temples to visit. Then head around the corner to embark on the Philosopher’s Walk that is lined with cherry blossoms. .

Restaurants

For the evening, go twalked over to Pontocho Alley, a famous narrow walkway in Gion filled with fancy restaurants and bars that house many different drinks and make for a posh evening out.

What to do in a week

  • Day 1/2: Arrive into Osaka International Airport and travel directly to Kyoto and follow the 2 day tour route.
  • Day 3: Take a trip to Nara to visit the deer and make sure not to take any food with you because the deer will try to take it.
  • Day 4: Take a train to see Hiroshima and the memorials there to experience the rich history of Japan
  • Day 5: Mount Fuji is on the way to Tokyo, so make sure you get off the train to see the beautiful mountain or even try your luck at skiing
  • Day 6/7:Spend the final two days in Tokyo to experience the hectic life of most Japanese people with bright lights and take time to explore the many districts
